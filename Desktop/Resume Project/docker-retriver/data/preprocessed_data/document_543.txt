Article 177: Rights of Ministers and Advocate-General as respects the Houses - Constitution of India Part VI Article 177 Rights of Ministers and Advocate-General as respects the Houses Every Minister and the Advocate-General for a State shall have the right to speak in, and otherwise to take part in the proceedings of, the Legislative Assembly of the State or, in the case of a State having a Legislative Council, both Houses, and to speak in, and otherwise to take part in the proceedings of, any committee of the Legislature of which he may be named a member, but shall not, by virtue of this article, be entitled to vote. Version 1 Article 156, Draft Constitution of India 1948 Every minister and the Advocate-General for a State shall have the right to speak in, and otherwise to take part in the proceedings of, the Legislative Assembly of the State or, in the case of a State having a Legislative Council, both Houses and any joint sitting of the Houses, and to speak in, and otherwise to take part in the proceedings of, any committee of the Legislature of which he may be named a member, but shall not, by virtue of this article, be entitled to vote. Version 2 Article 177, Constitution of India 1950 Every Minister and the Advocate-General for a State shall have the right to speak in, and otherwise to take part in the proceedings of, the Legislative Assembly of the State or, in the case of a State having a Legislative Council, both Houses, and to speak in, and otherwise to take part in the proceedings of, any committee of the Legislature of which he may be named a member, but shall not, by virtue of this article, be entitled to vote. Summary Draft Article 156 (Article 177) was debated on 2 June 1949. It gave the Advocate-General and all ministers of a State the right to participate in the proceedings of the State Legislature. The Draft Article was adopted without debate on 2 June 1949.
